Week 1 Pre-Session Primer: Cell Structure & Microscopy (Combined)

Welcome to Intensive A-Level Biology!

This primer will help you prepare for our first extended session covering both microscopy AND complete cell structure. Since we have only 33 weeks to cover everything, our first session combines what would normally be two separate topics. Don’t worry if you can’t answer everything - these questions are to get you thinking about the topics we’ll explore together.

Part A: Reflection Questions (Think about these)

Scale and Size - The Invisible World

  1. Look around the room. What’s the smallest thing you can see with your naked eyes?
  2. Why do you think we need microscopes to see cells?
  3. How many times bigger do you think a human hair is compared to a bacterial cell?
  4. If you could shrink down to the size of a molecule, what do you think the inside of a cell would look like?

Microscopy - Seeing the Unseeable

  1. Have you ever used a microscope? What did you observe and how clear was the image?
  2. Why do you think we can’t see viruses with a school light microscope?
  3. What’s the difference between making something look bigger and seeing more detail?
  4. If you wanted to see the surface of a pollen grain in 3D detail, what type of microscope might you need?

Cells - Life’s Building Blocks

  1. What do you think makes a cell “alive”?
  2. Why do you think some organisms are made of just one cell while others have trillions?
  3. What do you think are the most important parts of any cell?
  4. How do you think plant cells differ from animal cells, and why?

The Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Divide

  1. You’ve probably heard of bacteria. Do you think bacteria have the same internal structures as human cells?
  2. What advantages might there be to having a nucleus (like humans) vs not having one (like bacteria)?
  3. Why do you think antibiotics kill bacteria but not human cells?

Part B: Knowledge Check

Quick Microscopy Questions

  1. What does “magnification” mean?
  2. What does “resolution” mean?
  3. Name the two main types of electron microscope.
  4. Why can’t you observe living cells with an electron microscope?
  5. If the eyepiece is ×10 and the objective lens is ×40, what’s the total magnification?

Cell Structure Basics

  1. What does “prokaryotic” mean? What does “eukaryotic” mean?
  2. Name 5 structures you might find in an animal cell.
  3. Name 3 additional structures found in plant cells but not animal cells.
  4. What is DNA and where would you find it in different types of cells?
  5. What are ribosomes and why are they important?

Size and Scale

  1. Arrange these from smallest to largest: virus, bacterial cell, plant cell, animal cell, protein molecule
  2. Which is bigger - a mitochondrion or a nucleus?
  3. What size are typical bacterial cells? What about typical animal cells?

Function and Purpose

  1. Why do you think plant cells have a cell wall but animal cells don’t?
  2. How do you think nutrients get into a cell and waste gets out?
  3. Why might muscle cells have more mitochondria than skin cells?
  4. How do cells know when and how to divide?
  5. What do you think controls all the activities in a cell?

Part C: Practical Thinking & Calculations

Magnification Mathematics

  1. A cell appears 20mm long in a diagram. If the actual cell is 20μm, what is the magnification?

    • Hint: First convert units so they match!

  2. You’re observing bacteria at ×50,000 magnification. A bacterium measures 15cm in your image. Calculate its actual size in μm.

  3. An eyepiece graticule shows that a plant cell is 25 graticule units long. If 1 unit = 4μm at this magnification, what’s the actual cell length?

  4. Convert these measurements:

    • 2.5 mm to μm
    • 500 nm to μm
    • 0.05 mm to nm

Problem Solving - Choosing the Right Tool

  1. You want to observe living blood cells moving. Which microscope would you use?
      1. Light microscope
      1. Transmission electron microscope
      1. Scanning electron microscope
    • Why?
  2. You want to see the detailed internal structure of mitochondria. Which would be better?
      1. Light microscope at ×1000
      1. Transmission electron microscope
    • Why?
  3. You want to see the 3D surface structure of a bacterium. Which microscope?
      1. Light microscope
      1. TEM
      1. SEM
    • Why?

Cell Identification Challenge

  1. A scientist discovers a microscopic organism and observes:

    • No visible nucleus
    • Cell wall present
    • Very small (2 μm)
    • Simple internal structure

    Is this prokaryotic or eukaryotic? What clues tell you this?

  2. Under the microscope, you see a cell with:

    • Clear nucleus
    • Green structures (chloroplasts)
    • Thick cell wall
    • Large central space

    What type of cell is this? What are the green structures for?

Part D: Research Challenge (Optional but helpful)

Historical Discoveries

Using any resources (textbook, internet, videos), find out:

  1. The Cell Theory - What are the three main principles? Who were the key scientists?

  2. Microscopy Pioneers:

    • Robert Hooke - What did he discover and when?
    • Anton van Leeuwenhoek - What was he first to observe?
    • What did these discoveries prove about life?

  3. Size Comparison Activity - Research and arrange these from smallest to largest with approximate sizes:

    • Hydrogen atom
    • Glucose molecule
    • Protein molecule
    • Virus particle
    • Bacterial cell
    • Mitochondrion
    • Animal cell
    • Plant cell

Cell Structure Deep Dive

  1. Organelle Functions - Match these organelles to their main function and find out why each structure is shaped the way it is:
    • Mitochondria → ?
    • Nucleus → ?
    • Chloroplast → ?
    • Ribosome → ?
    • Cell membrane → ?
    • Golgi apparatus → ?
  2. Endosymbiotic Theory - This is advanced:
    • What does this theory suggest about how eukaryotic cells evolved?
    • What evidence supports it?
    • Why do mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA?

Real-World Applications

  1. Medical Applications:
    • How are electron microscopes used in hospitals?
    • Why is understanding cell structure important for treating diseases?
    • How do cancer cells differ from normal cells when viewed under a microscope?

Part E: Curiosity Questions (Just reflect on these)

Deep Thinking

  1. How do antibiotics kill bacteria but not human cells?
  2. Why can’t we build a light microscope that can see atoms?
  3. How do cells “know” what type of cell to become (muscle, nerve, skin, etc.)?
  4. What makes cancer cells different from normal cells?
  5. Could we ever create an artificial cell from scratch?
  6. How did the first cell evolve if all cells come from other cells?
  7. Why are most cells so small? Why don’t we have cells the size of tennis balls?

Future Possibilities

  1. What new types of microscopy might be developed in the future?
  2. How might understanding cell structure help us develop new medicines?

Part F: Drawing Practice

On separate pieces of paper, try to draw what you think these look like (don’t worry about being perfect):

  1. A typical bacterial cell - Label any parts you know
  2. A typical animal cell - Label any parts you know
  3. A typical plant cell - Label any parts you know
  4. What you think the inside of a mitochondrion looks like
  5. How you imagine different microscopes work (simple diagrams)

Scale Drawing Challenge

  1. If a bacterial cell is 2 μm long and an animal cell is 20 μm long, draw them to scale next to each other. What do you notice?
